Packaging as brand statement: what the new wave of minimalism signals

The supermarket is one of the most studied retail environments in marketing — and one of the least noticed by the people inside it. Every fixture, every scent, every product placement has been thought about. The question is whether you notice it thinking about you back.

The dwell-time economy

UK supermarkets have shifted their layout logic in the last few years — not dramatically, but deliberately. The goal is no longer just to move volume. It is to slow you down. Longer dwell time correlates with higher basket size, and the store design now reflects that.

Bakeries moved to the back. Flowers moved to the entrance. These are not accidents — they are sensory anchors. The smell of bread draws you deeper into the store. The flowers signal freshness at the point of first impression.

What this means for brands

If you are a brand selling through supermarket channels, your packaging is competing inside a designed environment that already has an agenda. Understanding that environment — the foot traffic patterns, the fixture logic, the lighting — is as important as the product itself.

This is something I observed repeatedly during my time watching retail environments as part of my MSc research into consumer behaviour. The shelf is not neutral. It is already making an argument before your product says a word.
